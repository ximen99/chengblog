1. Introduction¶

Some personal background story

It has always been one of my main interest in making sense of the world based on statistical findings, which greatly satisfies my curiosities as well as my passion for math. During my recent employment in PE fund for primary equity market, one of my main duty is to study industry and business trends and to derive guidelines in selecting best investment opportunities. However unless there are large listed companies in the target industry, a lot of times key industry and business data are protected as trade secrets and are not openly available, thus the more effective ways in doing research during work are through conversing with business insiders and industry experts and through field trip study, as oppose to deriving the conclusions through statistical findings. On the other hand, trade data and financial data are very comprehensive and readily available in the secondary equity market, so I have kept it as a hobby to play with the data, devise and back test quantative investment models. Through time I have created a few models that I feel confident to use in real-time stock market. Partnered with a friend, we entered into a business venture to form a PE fund that invest in mainland China stock market with those models. Even though the business venture didnt end up in success, I had great fun in the process. Those studies also turn out to be great exercise in improving my coding and modeling capabilities. This report is based on the result of my study in devising a market timing model for the PE fund business venture.

Where the inspiration came from

The argument that whether we are able to perform market timing that leads to better investment return than simple buy and hold or dollar cost averaging strategy, may have exsisted since the beginning of stock market. The same question has baffled me since I first got into finance, so as soon as I learned how to code and backtest models, I have tried all the popular technical indicators and other market timing strategies on mainland China stock indices. It turned out there're several trend following technical indicators, particularly MA20, MACD, can improve investment return and reduce the drawdown, at least in stock market in mainland China, however those indicators tend to perform poorly in dull market and the signals tend to lag the trend quite a bit. There should be a way to better detect and reflect current market conditions.

There's a saying that "market always move in the path of the least resistance". If we can better detect the changes of current resistance and support, maybe we can better predict the future price direction. A traditional technical analyst or chart analyst will compare recent lows and recent highs to detect how resistance and support level change. By Quantifying the changes through mathematical equations, we should be able to build a model to automate the process and accomplish the same thing based on this idea. This is the main thought process behind the scene.

# define a function to calculate Strategy NAV
def cal_NAV(hold_status,StrategyName):
    Strategy_NAV = (price_df.daily_return[hold_status.index]*hold_status.astype('int64')+1).cumprod()
    CSI300_NAV = (price_df.daily_return[hold_status.index]+1).cumprod()
    return pd.DataFrame({
        StrategyName:Strategy_NAV,
        'CSI300':CSI300_NAV
    }).reset_index().melt('index')

Quantify Resistance and Support

As mentioned above, according to traditional technical analysis the key to predict price direction is to evaluate the changes in resistance and support level. If at current price level support is stronger than resistance, we should expect price to go up and vice versa. If we look at the intraday market, the lowest point can be the support level of the day and the highest point can be the resistance level of the day. Now to quantify the changes in the strength of resistance and support, we just need to regress the lows against the highs of each day for N periods of days, where N should be large enough to average out the noise in daily price and small enough to be responsive to changes in market condition.

High = alpha + beta*low + epsilon, epsilon ~ N(0,sigma)

When beta is large, it means support is stronger than resistance, we can say price is likely to go up and vice versa. To illustrate my point.Lets see what high beta and low beta price action looks like in uptrending and down trending market.

In [10]:
fit_period = 18
for i in range(fit_period-1,len(price_df)):
    df=price_df.iloc[i-fit_period+1:i+1]
    model = sml.ols(formula='high~low',data = df)
    result=model.fit()
    price_df.loc[df.iloc[-1].name,'beta'] = result.params[1]
    price_df.loc[df.iloc[-1].name,'R2'] = result.rsquared

Now we have the beta value for each day. We can come up with a strategy that hold stock when beta is over 1 and sell when beta is below 0.8. The threshold levels are set based on historical data, however in real world since the market condition is constantly changing, the standard deviation and mean for beta is changing as well. if we plot the moving average of beta value for the past year we can see its varying by pretty large degree.

One way to solve the problem is to normalize the data and to include a full bull and bear market cycle let's use the past five years data to do the calculation. And sometimes in unusual period of time the price will move very dramatically due to news impact such as the trade war between US and China. Since SML model are sensitive to outliers, these data point tend to have a big effect on the beta as well. In order to increase the robustness of the indicator, we can penalize those data point by multiplying the R squared.

beta_mean = price_df.beta.rolling(window = norm_period).mean()

As we can see there's a clear positive correlation between our indicator and stock return, however the monotonicity is not good enough. Especially on the left side of the index, when the indicator value decreases we don't see a clear declining trend in stock return. To fix this problem, one idea is to amplify the monotonicity one the right side of the scale. To accomplish that we can multiply our adjusted beta score with beta value again and make the new indicator positively skewed.

3. Model Testing¶

We'll set the buy and sell signal threshold to 0.7 and -0.7. When the indicator value cross over 0.7 triger buy signal and hold the stock till the indicator cross below -0.7.
